# Book Philosophy

## Purpose

Book pages document ideas that have influenced my engineering thinking.

They answer a specific question:

> **"How has this book influenced the engineer I am becoming?"**

They do not answer:

> "What is this book about?"

Visitors can find summaries elsewhere.

Book pages explain why a book matters to my engineering journey, what ideas I carried forward, and where those ideas appear throughout the knowledge system.

---

# Not a Bookshelf

Book pages are not a reading catalogue.

They are not a review site.

They are not reading statistics.

They are not Goodreads.

Every book recorded has shaped my thinking in some way.

Since my engineering thinking is inseparable from who I am, every recorded book belongs within the engineering knowledge system — even if the subject is not explicitly technical.

Fiction, philosophy, science, history — any book that changed how I think about problems, systems, or engineering belongs here.

---

# Influence over Summary

Book pages should primarily communicate influence.

What did I learn?

What did I change?

What did I build differently?

What problems did I approach differently after reading this?

The authored content should connect ideas to engineering practice rather than restate the book's content.

---

# Relationships over Reviews

Relationships are more valuable than ratings.

A book connected to three projects, a workflow, and a technology page communicates more than any star rating.

The visitor should naturally discover how a book's ideas propagated through projects, workflows, professional work, and engineering decisions.

---

# Exploration

Every Book page should encourage exploration throughout the rest of the website.

Someone arriving on a Book page should leave by exploring:

* projects influenced by the book's ideas
* technologies the book introduced
* workflows shaped by the book's philosophy
* related books that extend the ideas

Books are entry points into the knowledge system, not endpoints.

---

# Curation

Not every book read is worth recording.

Only books that meaningfully influenced engineering thinking should appear.

This is intentional curation rather than completeness.

Quality of influence matters more than quantity of books read.

---

# Reading Status

Unlike Technologies (which have no lifecycle), Books have a natural reading lifecycle:

* reading — currently being read
* completed — finished reading
* abandoned — stopped reading intentionally

This lifecycle is factual and objective.

It does not rate the book.

It does not measure the reader.

It describes the current state of the reading relationship.

---

# No Ratings

Book pages should never contain:

* star ratings
* numeric scores
* ranked lists
* reading challenges
* gamification
* pages-per-day statistics
* reading speed metrics

These belong on social reading platforms, not in an engineering knowledge system.

Influence is demonstrated through relationships and authored reflection, not through quantification.

---

# Living Documents

Book pages may evolve.

As engineering experience grows, the influence of a book may become clearer over time.

New projects may reveal connections that were not obvious during initial reading.

Book pages should be updated when new relationships emerge.

---

# Integration

Books are Knowledge Nodes within the same system as Projects, Technologies, and Workflows.

They participate in:

* the `related` relationship model
* backlinks
* automatic discovery (when other pages reference them)
* navigation
* exploration

They share the same partials, styling conventions, and architectural patterns as every other Knowledge Node.

---

# Engineering Context

Books contribute to understanding the engineer behind the projects.

A visitor exploring the knowledge system should encounter books naturally alongside technologies, workflows, and professional experience.

Together, these form a complete picture of engineering thinking — not just what was built, but what shaped the builder.
