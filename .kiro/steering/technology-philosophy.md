# Technology Philosophy

## Purpose

Technology pages are the central knowledge hubs of the website.

Unlike Projects, Workflows or Work Experience—which document moments in my engineering journey—Technology pages document the engineering concepts, tools, languages, platforms and standards that connect those experiences together.

They answer a different question:

> **"What role has this technology played throughout my engineering journey?"**

The purpose is not to explain what a technology is.

Visitors can find that elsewhere.

Instead, Technology pages explain my relationship with the technology, how I have applied it, how my understanding has evolved and where it appears throughout the knowledge system.

---

# Knowledge Hub

Technology pages are the primary cross-linking hubs of the website.

Every Knowledge Node should eventually connect to Technologies.

Examples include:

* Projects
* Workflows
* Work Experience
* Education
* Blog Posts
* Project Journals

Technology pages automatically aggregate these relationships while also providing manually authored context.

---

# Manual First

Technology pages are **not** automatically generated taxonomy pages.

They are manually authored Knowledge Nodes.

Automatic discovery enriches the page but never replaces human-written content.

The page should primarily communicate engineering experience rather than metadata.

---

# Relationship over Definition

Technology pages should not become miniature Wikipedia articles.

Instead of explaining what MQTT or Rust are, explain:

* why I chose them,
* where I learned them,
* where I use them,
* what problems they solve for me,
* what tradeoffs I have experienced,
* how my understanding has changed.

Relationships are more valuable than definitions.

---

# Evidence over Self-Rating

Technology pages should never contain subjective proficiency ratings.

Avoid:

* stars
* percentages
* beginner/intermediate/expert labels
* lifecycle statuses borrowed from projects

Instead, demonstrate experience through evidence.

Examples include:

* projects
* workflows
* professional experience
* education
* journal entries
* articles
* timeline events

Visitors should naturally infer familiarity through the body of work.

---

# No Project Lifecycle

Technology pages do not participate in the shared project status system.

Projects have lifecycles: they move from planning through active development to completion or archival.

Technologies have relationships that evolve continuously.

A technology is never "completed".

A technology is never "under active development" in the way a project is.

Applying project statuses to technologies encourages false lifecycle thinking and implies the relationship will eventually end.

Technology pages use the Living Documents model instead: they grow richer as evidence accumulates.

---

# Metadata Philosophy

Technology metadata should describe objective, factual properties of the relationship.

Appropriate metadata:

* **category** — what kind of technology this is (language, tool, platform, framework, standard)
* **firstUsed** — when the relationship began (approximate year or date)
* **favorite** — personal preference signal (boolean, optional)
* **dailyDriver** — currently part of the active engineering toolbox (boolean, optional)
* **relatedTechnologies** — manually curated connections to other technology pages

Inappropriate metadata:

* status (lifecycle state)
* proficiency level
* experience years as a skill metric
* confidence rating

The distinction: appropriate metadata describes facts about the relationship.

Inappropriate metadata attempts to measure or rate the engineer.

---

## Favorite

A technology marked as favorite communicates personal affinity.

It means: "I enjoy working with this and would choose it when appropriate."

It does not measure skill.

It should be visually indicated but not ranked.

---

## Daily Driver

A technology marked as dailyDriver communicates current, active use.

It means: "This is part of my regular engineering practice right now."

Daily driver status is expected to change over time.

It replaces the misleading `status = "active"` with a specific, factual claim.

Technologies that are no longer daily drivers are not "archived" — they simply aren't part of the current toolbox.

---

## Category

Category classifies the type of technology rather than the relationship.

Examples:

* language
* tool
* platform
* framework
* library
* standard
* protocol
* hardware

This supports filtering and grouping on list pages.

---

# Living Documents

Technology pages evolve over time.

They are expected to grow alongside engineering experience.

As projects, work experience and workflows evolve, the corresponding Technology pages become richer through additional relationships and updated reflections.

---

# Shared Knowledge

Technology pages should help visitors discover new parts of the website.

Someone arriving on a Technology page should naturally continue exploring projects, workflows, journals and experience connected to that technology.

Every Technology page should strengthen the interconnected nature of the knowledge system.

The goal is not documentation.

The goal is exploration.
