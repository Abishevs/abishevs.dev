# Rewrite number 2

## How I Use AI—and Whether I’m Doing Too Much Myself

I finally started writing more regularly and publishing my thoughts.
Surprisingly, it has been enjoyable.

At the same time, I find myself conflicted about artificial intelligence. I am
tired of AI-generated content, tired of the endless stream of polished but
generic text that seems to fill every corner of the internet. Yet I also
recognize that I have become deeply accustomed to using AI myself.

For nearly three years, I have used AI almost every day. Sometimes it helps with
programming. Other times it serves as a sounding board for technical ideas,
questions about hardware, or curiosity-driven rabbit holes. I might spend an
hour discussing how a sensor works, exploring a potential project, or testing an
idea that appeared out of nowhere.

Recently, however, I have felt the need to create more and consume less. The
irony is that my response to spending too much time talking to chatbots is to
write more blog posts. Instead of relying on AI to organize every thought, I
want to spend more time developing and expressing ideas on my own.

## AI Is Everywhere

AI has become embedded in nearly every aspect of online life. While there are
many benefits, one trend concerns me: the growing volume of AI-generated content
in open-source communities and professional spaces.

In open-source projects, this often appears as AI-assisted documentation
contributions. Some contributions are useful, but the sheer volume raises
questions about quality, originality, and long-term value. Documentation exists
to help people learn and understand systems. If large portions of it become
generic AI-generated text, the result may be technically correct while lacking
the clarity, context, and insight that come from genuine experience.

The same pattern appears across social media platforms. Increasingly, I
encounter posts that feel unmistakably AI-generated. The grammar is flawless.
The structure is polished. Every paragraph flows perfectly into the next. Yet
something feels missing.

The challenge is not that the writing is bad. In many cases, it is objectively
well written. The problem is that it often feels detached from a real person.

## The Loss of Personality Online

There was a time when imperfections in writing revealed something about the
individual behind the words. People wrote differently, made mistakes, followed
unusual trains of thought, and expressed ideas in ways that reflected their
personalities.

Today, much of online communication seems optimized for appearance. Content is
expected to look professional, polished, and authoritative. As AI tools become
more common, it becomes increasingly easy to produce text that meets those
expectations.

The result is a growing sense of uniformity.

Of course, there are still exceptions. Many people continue to create because
they genuinely care about what they are making. They experiment, learn, and
spend countless hours pursuing ideas simply because they find them interesting.
They build projects that nobody asked for, explore strange questions, and follow
their curiosity wherever it leads.

That kind of creativity often emerges from boredom, reflection, and unstructured
thinking. It requires time and mental space. Yet modern technology, social
media, and increasingly AI-driven workflows compete for that space. We are
constantly presented with new information, new content, and new distractions.

As a result, opportunities for genuine curiosity can become harder to find.

## How We Want to Be Perceived

Lately, I have noticed a broader pattern in both myself and others: we care
deeply about how our work is perceived.

Many people would rather publish an AI-generated article than risk being seen as
poor writers. That impulse is understandable. AI can instantly improve grammar,
structure, and readability. It removes many of the weaknesses that people feel
self-conscious about.

But there is a trade-off.

Human writing is rarely perfect. Ideas emerge unevenly. Arguments wander before
finding their conclusion. People make mistakes, revise their thinking, and
occasionally express themselves awkwardly. Those imperfections are often what
make writing feel authentic.

When every sentence is optimized, something human can disappear in the process.

The paradox is that the very qualities we often try to eliminate—our rough
edges, uncertainties, and individual voices—are frequently what make our ideas
worth reading in the first place.

As AI becomes a standard tool for communication, the challenge may not be
learning how to use it effectively. The challenge may be preserving enough of
ourselves in the process that our work still feels like it came from a real
person.
